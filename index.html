Towards 3D-printing of mechanically-realistic kidney models for surgical training, planning and rehearsal

Lisa Asciak1, Will Shu1
1Department of Biomedical Engineering, University of Strathclyde, Glasgow, UK
1
Introduction

The use of 3D printed artificial organ models is gradually gaining acceptance in medicine, especially for surgical training and planning. However, current 3D printing technology cannot accurately replicate patient-specific anatomical complexities and material properties, as well as the capability to produce realistic organ models with minimal ethical and regulatory implications.

To date, most 3D printed organ models have been predominantly fabricated using commercially available printers, typically resulting in hard and rigid structures that only coincidentally match human soft tissue, therefore limiting the realism of the 3D printed model. To address the material property mismatch, some studies have exploited the benefits of 3D printing to produce anatomically accurate models and soft gel materials, such as silicone and hydrogels, to mimic biological phantoms. However, this method can be costly and time-consuming. Therefore, there is a need to develop fully 3D printed models using materials that mimic the biomechanical complexity of human soft tissue is required.

In this work we aim to develop a hydrogel formulation compatible with Digital Light Processing (DLP) 3D printing for fully 3D printed soft organ models, specifically a kidney and its tissue-like material.

Methods
(1) Resin preparation and hydrogel 3D-printing

A DLP 3D printer (Asiga® - PICO2) was used to fabricate alginate-based interpenetrating polymer network (IPN) hydrogels. The resin was prepared by mixing PEGDA (20% w/v), alginate (2% w/v), photoinitiator (0.25% w/v), and distilled water. Following 3D printing, the structures were immersed in a calcium chloride solution (0.5M) for 24h for alginate crosslinking.

(2) Material mechanical characterisation

The different hydrogel formulations (0% NP, 10% NP, 20% NP and 30% NP) were mechanically characterised via uniaxial tensile and compression to failure tests at a rate of 1mm/min and 5mm/s, respectively (Mach-1, Biomomentum Inc.).

Viscoelastic behaviour was quantified via rheological measurements (Kinexus Pro+ rotational rheometer, NETZSCH), including a frequency sweep from 0.1 to 10 Hz at a fixed shear strain of 0.01%.

Conclusions & Future Work
  • An alginate-based IPN hydrogel formulation compatible with DLP 3D-printing was developed exhibiting tunable mechanical properties depending on monomer concentration.
  • The 20% and 30% IPN hydrogels showed superior mechanical properties when compared with the single-networked alginate hydrogels.
  • Scaled-down kidney structures based on patient kidney scans obtained from open repositories were successfully printed.
Future Work
  • Attempts at scaling up the kidney structures to match the geometrical dimensions of the human kidney
  • Utilising patient-specific data to 3D print kidney models with specific kidney conditions
  • Include larger inner channels within the hydrogels to mimic the hollow kidney vasculature and collecting system
  • Perform non-invasive interactions to assess surgical application suitability
